AI-Driven Early Diabetes Risk Prediction Using Clinical, Lifestyle & Wearable Data

"This project predicts the risk of Type 2 diabetes at an early stage using machine learning models trained on clinical indicators, lifestyle behaviors, and wearable device data to enable preventive healthcare interventions."

Problem Statement

Diabetes often remains undiagnosed until serious complications develop due to the slow and asymptomatic progression of Type 2 diabetes. Existing diagnostic methods rely on clinical blood tests that are reactive, invasive, and dependent on healthcare infrastructure.

This diagnostic gap results in millions of undetected cases and delayed treatment, increasing the risk of cardiovascular disease, kidney failure, nerve damage, and vision loss.

This project develops an AI-based diabetes risk prediction system that uses non-invasive health indicators, lifestyle behaviors, and wearable device data to identify high-risk individuals before clinical diagnosis, enabling proactive healthcare and early intervention.

Literature Review

Early prediction of diabetes has attracted significant research attention due to its potential to reduce long-term complications through preventive intervention.

Smith et al. (2022), "Machine Learning for Early Diabetes Diagnosis: A Comparative Study of Ensemble Methods," demonstrated that ensemble learning models significantly outperform traditional statistical approaches. Their findings showed improved sensitivity and specificity when Random Forest and boosting techniques were applied to health datasets.

Chen & Guestrin (2016), "XGBoost: A Scalable Tree Boosting System," introduced the XGBoost algorithm, which has become a benchmark for structured health data prediction due to its regularization capabilities and high predictive accuracy.

Chen et al. (2023), "The Impact of Feature Engineering on Predictive Accuracy in Chronic Disease Models," highlighted that incorporating lifestyle and behavioral features significantly improves predictive performance and model generalization.

Traditional statistical approaches remain clinically relevant. Hosmer & Lemeshow (2000), Applied Logistic Regression, established Logistic Regression as a reliable baseline model due to its interpretability and acceptance in medical research.

Explainability is critical in medical AI systems. Lundberg & Lee (2017), "A Unified Approach to Interpreting Model Predictions," introduced SHAP, a framework that explains machine learning predictions and improves transparency and clinical trust.

Recent advancements also emphasize continuous monitoring. Piwek et al. (2016), "The Rise of Consumer Health Wearables," highlighted the potential of wearable devices to collect continuous physiological data such as heart rate, sleep patterns, and physical activity, enabling proactive disease risk assessment.

Collectively, these studies demonstrate that combining ensemble machine learning models, lifestyle-based features, wearable data, and explainable AI can significantly enhance early diabetes risk prediction.
